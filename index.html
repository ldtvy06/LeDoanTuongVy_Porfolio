PROJECT 01
Veilmark

visible to the right people

A B2B SCHOOL MERCHANDISE IDENTITY SYSTEM

Safety Symbol School Branding

The Tension

Many schools use customized merchandise systems to manage student belongings while reinforcing school identity and consistency.

They use visible names to identify children's belongings quickly and efficiently.

However, public name visibility may unintentionally expose children's personal information outside school environments.

Real-World Observation

This insight emerged after seeing a parenting video where a mother shared why she stopped printing her child's name on personal belongings after a stranger called the child correctly in public.

The child immediately responded — highlighting how familiar cues such as names can unintentionally create a false sense of trust and familiarity.

Identification creates convenience —
but also visibility.

Consumer Insight

Schools and parents both want kids' names on merch —

schools for identification, parents for personalization —

but parents fear it's also a stranger's first introduction to their child.

"Risk elimination is unrealistic. Risk reduction through controlled visibility is achievable."

VEILMARK – A B2B School Merchandise Identity System

A Dual Identity System

Public-facing layerSymbol-based identification

Internal layerStudent name / ID information

With this system, the risk of children's information being leaked is significantly reduced.

Design Principles — The 3S Framework
Safety

Protecting children's information through controlled visibility.

Symbol

Creating recognizable and emotionally engaging personal identifiers.

School Branding

Building unified and differentiated school identity systems.

Strategic Value

Schools – Branding + differentiation
Parents – Safety + peace of mind
Children – Recognition + belonging

Go-to-Market Strategy

Target Partners

  • Private preschools
  • Kindergartens
  • Education centers
  • Primary schools

⇒ Targeting schools seeking stronger branding consistency and modernized student merchandise systems.

Rollout Approach

01 – Pilot

Launch with selected classrooms using themed identity systems.

02 — Feedback

Collect parent, teacher, and operational feedback.

03 — Scale

Expand into school-wide merchandise and branding systems.

*Long-term opportunity: School-wide identity ecosystems

Beyond Identification

Veilmark rethinks how schools identify children by balancing:

  • safer recognition
  • emotional personalization
  • operational convenience
  • school branding

FINAL STATEMENT

We help schools build a stronger brand identity while keeping every child safely recognized.

PROJECT 03 - Academic Project • Public Dataset Analysis

ILIA BEAUTY

Customer Insights & Churn Analytics

A customer analytics project combining SPSS and Power BI to explore purchasing behavior, identify churn patterns, and generate actionable business insights.

Power BI SPSS Customer Analytics Churn Analysis N = 250,000

Business Context

Business
Question

Which customer segments and purchasing behaviors are linked to churn, and what insights can improve retention?

Dataset Source: Synthetic E-commerce Customer Behavior and Purchase Dataset (Kaggle), generated using the Faker Python library for educational and analytical purposes.

Objectives

  • 01 Analyze customer purchasing behavior across demographic and product segments
  • 02 Identify churn-prone customer segments through statistical testing
  • 03 Explore relationships between demographics, payment behavior, and spending
  • 04 Generate data-driven recommendations for improving customer retention

Analytical Highlights

Churn Rate

The percentage of customers who stop purchasing from the brand during the analysis period.

20.05%

Retention Rate

The percentage of customers who continue buying from the brand over time.

79.95%

Average Purchase Value

The average amount spent per transaction across all customers.

2,725

Monthly Revenue Trend

The change in revenue across months to monitor growth or decline.

↓ Sep–Nov

Return Rate

The percentage of orders that are returned by customers.

50%

Methodology

Analysis Process

01
Data Exploration

Reviewed dataset structure, checked for missing values, and validated distributions across 250,000 customer records. Variables included age, gender, product category, payment method, purchase amount, returns, and churn status.

02
Descriptive Analysis

Generated frequency tables and descriptive statistics in SPSS to profile the customer base — age range 18–70, average purchase ~2,725, and an even gender split. Churn rate confirmed at 20.1%.

03
Statistical Analysis

Applied chi-square tests of independence to assess associations between churn and categorical variables (gender, product category, payment method, age group). Independent samples t-tests were used for continuous variables (age, purchase amount).

04
Dashboard Development

Built an interactive Power BI dashboard featuring KPI cards, revenue trend line, churn breakdowns by segment, and customer distribution charts — enabling stakeholder exploration by product category, gender, and age group.

Results & Insights

Key
Findings

Statistical tests run across 5 variables. Only 1 categorical variable — payment method — showed a significant association with churn.

Finding 01 Consistent

Churn rate is stable at ~20% with no strong demographic protection

Churn is fairly consistent across the customer base — gender (p=.169) and product category (p=.910) show no statistically significant association with churn behavior.

Finding 02 Significant p=.003

Payment method is the only variable significantly linked to churn

Chi-square test confirms a statistically significant relationship between payment method and churn (χ²=11.909, p=.003). This is the strongest actionable signal in the dataset.

χ²=11.909 · df=2 · p=.003

Finding 03 Mixed signal

Age is associated with churn distribution, but not mean age difference

Chi-square shows a significant distribution across age groups (χ²=386.910, p<.001), but the mean age between churned and retained customers does not differ significantly (t=1.141, p=.254) — suggesting age alone is not a reliable churn predictor.

χ²=386.910 · t=1.141 · p=.254

Finding 04 Not significant

Gender and product category provide limited evidence for churn-based segmentation

Neither gender (p=.169) nor product category (p=.910) show a statistically significant relationship with churn. Retention strategies built on these segments lack statistical support.

Gender p=.169 · Product p=.910

Finding 05 Not significant

Spending level does not distinguish churned from retained customers

Purchase amount does not differ significantly between churned and retained customers (t=-.353, p=.724). A mean difference of ~2.5 on a 100–5350 scale has no practical economic significance.

t=-.353 · Mean diff=2.5 · p=.724

Finding 06 Trend alert

Revenue declined sharply from ~63M to ~46M between August and November

A significant drop in monthly revenue from August (~63M) to November (~46M) suggests a possible seasonal pattern or operational issue. Revenue partially recovered in December to ~47M.

Aug 63M → Nov 46M → Dec 47M

Recommendations

R1

Focus on the payment experience

Since payment method is the only variable with a statistically significant relationship to churn, analyze which payment options are associated with higher churn rates, and optimize the checkout UX for those segments.

R2

Avoid gender and product category as primary retention segments

Retention efforts should prioritize behavioral signals rather than simple demographic or category-based segmentation, which show no statistically significant relationship with churn.

R3

Investigate the September–November revenue decline

This drop may be related to seasonality, marketing campaign timing, or competition — and should be cross-checked with marketing and operational data to identify the root cause.

R4

Develop a retention monitoring dashboard

Create a dashboard that continuously tracks churn rate, revenue trends, and customer segments, enabling earlier detection of retention risks and supporting data-driven decision making.
